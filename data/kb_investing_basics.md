## Investing basics

Investing means using your money to buy assets that you expect to grow in value or produce income over time. Common investment types include:

- **Stocks** – shares of ownership in a company. Values can rise and fall quickly.
- **Bonds** – loans to governments or companies. Typically lower risk and lower return than stocks.
- **Exchange-traded funds (ETFs)** – baskets of securities you can buy in a single trade, often tracking an index.
- **Mutual funds** – pooled investments managed by a professional fund manager.

Key ideas:

- **Risk and return** – Assets with higher potential returns usually come with higher risk of loss.
- **Time horizon** – How long until you need the money. Longer horizons often allow more risk.
- **Diversification** – Spreading investments across different assets, sectors, and regions to reduce the impact of any single investment.

For most beginners, low-cost, diversified funds (such as index funds or broad-market ETFs) are a common way to start learning about investing while managing risk, but you should always research products and fees carefully.
